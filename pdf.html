Introduction

IP Explorer provides a report on your intangible assets. Intangibles can be divided into 3 main types:

  1. Intellectual Property (IP). Non-physical assets with special legal rights which protect your business model. You can use these rights to stop copying by others. Examples are trademarks and copyrights.
  2. Intellectual Assets. Identifiable assets that you can legally own, use, trade and/or license. These may have some legal protection. Examples are contracts and awards.
  3. Intellectual Capital. Assets you don’t “own” but benefit from using. Examples are your team’s know-how and skills.

This report identifies your intellectual property and intellectual assets. It aims to help you do three things:

  • Find the IP in your business
  • Protect your differentiators
  • Make best use of all your assets

Registered Intellectual Property

Formally registering your IP can increase and protect value in a business, and is worth considering whenever possible.

Registered rights help to create a sustainable and defensible barrier to entry for your competitors by providing you with a monopoly over the use of a particular name, mark, invention or design. For example, the 'first to file' trade mark system in Indonesia means the first person to apply gets the legal rights to a trademark.

A new invention or design that is not filed quickly will become unprotectable, owing to the official criteria that govern the application process (see below). Licensing will be much easier if your rights are formally recognized. Investors take considerable comfort from the presence of registered IP rights, and lenders are becoming increasingly aware of the role they play in protecting cashflows.

Patents, trade marks and designs all have to be officially registered at Dirjen Haki – Indonesia’s IP office https://www.dgip.go.id/. Designs and patents can only be registered if they are new and have not been disclosed (subject to a few defined exceptions, such as under an NDA). So, if you have not registered any invention or design now, but you already used or disclosed it (for example, by putting it on public display), it is no longer “novel” and cannot be registered. What this means is whenever a new invention or design is created, you need to consider whether you should register it immediately. If a patent application is successful, and your patent is granted, you will have annual maintenance fees to pay which increases over time.

Almost every company uses a distinctive name and many also have a distinctive logo. Some companies have slogans, sounds, colours or other marks as well as domain names that they use to help customers identify their products from those offered by competitors. All of these things can be registered as trade marks. Registering a trade mark is relatively inexpensive and makes it much easier to stop others using confusingly similar marks to you. Trade marks also make it much easier to license your products and allow you to use the ® mark next to your name. Unlike patents and designs, trademarks can be registered at any time; however, the risk is that if you do not register early, others may register first and may force you to stop using a name you prefer. The best practice is to search to check no one has a prior similar mark registered, then file early on. Remember to renew your trade marks every 10 years.

Copyright does not have to be registered in order to exist. To be able to enforce your copyright, the most important evidence to retain is proof of your creative process, i.e. the workings leading up to the final version. Official registration is possible in Indonesia; it is generally recommended only to register the most important works that you will continue to use for a long time in the same form, or where you are aware there is a possible dispute.

It is important that that all IP is owned by the business. It is common to own IP in personal names of shareholders and directors in Indonesia, but this is not a good business practice. It creates additional costs and risks, especially if the IP needs to be enforced, or someone accuses your business of infringement (you may end up being personally liable). Also, IP not owned by the business may not be counted by investors. Technically, license agreements must be signed and recorded with those individuals (but in practice this is costly and rare). All businesses should carefully check and if necessary, assign to the business all IP not held in the business’ name using a short contract, which then has to be registered at Dirjen Haki.

There are two purposes to registering IP. One is to protect your business model. It is usually worth analysing the IP you have registered in the light of your current trading activities. Is everything you offer protected in some way? Having the right IP registrations gives you a way to stop third parties interfering with your business.

The second purpose is to stop others from copying you. You should at least consider this and decide how you want to respond. If you don’t have the right IP registrations, you may not be able to take any action against them at all.

You may also have Artistic works such as photographs and graphics made specially for your business. If you have none of these, you should definitely consider creating some or all of them. This extra IP will strengthen your business model and boost your value.

To protect your copyright, it is good practice to display the copyright symbol (©) and wording such as 'Copyright [organisation name] [date]. All rights reserved' on all relevant materials, including websites and all printed documentation.

The most common mistake made by business with copyright is failing to own it properly. By default, every copyright work created is owned by its author or creator(s). There are two exceptions. Firstly, where a company employee created it, and their employment contract contains a provision that all IP is owned by the company. Secondly, where an independent contractor or designer created it, but there is a contract with that person which specifically assigns ownership to the business.

Business owners should get into the habit of checking that each contract has this provision, called an IP assignment clause. If you have not done so, go back through previous contracts where works have been created for you. If there is no agreement in place, ask the author to sign a very simple document assigning the copyright to your business.

One other risk to guard against is the possibility that you are infringing other people’s copyright, and that they could sue you. It is very easy to take material from the internet and re-use it, but doing so may get your business in trouble – always check the licensing conditions for assets like images. It is especially important to ensure that software you develop, or ask others to develop for you, does not ‘borrow’ code or other content from other parties that are not freely available under open source licences.

Contracts

The Contracts section covers all the types of legally binding arrangements that are relevant for your business. All businesses depend on contracts; usually these will be written, covering all the terms that have been agreed, and will be signed by both parties. You should not start trading with another business (even pay a deposit) until you have a signed contract – this is the most common source of disputes. If you are dealing directly with the public you should normally have written terms and conditions that set out what you are agreeing to provide.

Even without a written document, a contract can still exist – it may be oral or implied - but that is far less secure and not as valuable. So, it is recommended that all contracts are done properly – in writing, covering all the terms and signed by both parties. It is possible to have them created after an event – this is recommended if you have forgotten to do it at the time. Some special times of contracts may need to be registered – for land, for IP etc.
